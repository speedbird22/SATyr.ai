Main Ideas

Identifying Central Themes

The main idea is the central point or message that the author wants to convey. It's the most important concept that ties the entire passage together.

Look for topic sentences, usually at the beginning of paragraphs
Identify recurring themes and concepts
Pay attention to the conclusion, which often restates the main idea
Consider what the author spends the most time discussing

Distinguishing Main Ideas from Details

Main ideas are broad concepts, while supporting details are specific examples, facts, or explanations that support the main idea.

Main ideas answer "What is this passage mostly about?"
Details answer "How?" "Why?" or "What examples support this?"
Main ideas are general; details are specific
Example: Identifying Main Ideas

Passage: "Climate change poses significant threats to global food security. Rising temperatures affect crop yields, while changing precipitation patterns disrupt growing seasons. Additionally, extreme weather events destroy harvests and agricultural infrastructure. These factors combine to create food shortages and price increases worldwide."

Main Idea: Climate change threatens global food security.

Supporting Details: Rising temperatures, changing precipitation, extreme weather events, food shortages, price increases.

Main Idea Tip

If you can't identify the main idea immediately, try summarizing each paragraph in one sentence, then look for the common thread connecting them all.

Supporting Details

Types of Supporting Details

Supporting details provide evidence, examples, and explanations that strengthen the main idea:

Facts and statistics that provide concrete evidence
Examples and anecdotes that illustrate concepts
Expert opinions and quotations
Comparisons and contrasts
Cause and effect relationships

Evaluating Detail Relevance

Not all details are equally important. Learn to distinguish between:

Primary details: directly support the main idea
Secondary details: support primary details
Irrelevant details: interesting but don't support the main point
Example: Analyzing Supporting Details

Main Idea: Regular exercise provides numerous health benefits.

Primary Detail: Exercise reduces the risk of heart disease by 30%.

Secondary Detail: This reduction occurs because exercise strengthens the heart muscle.

Irrelevant Detail: The first modern gym opened in 1847.

Using Details for Comprehension

Supporting details help you:

Understand complex concepts through examples
Evaluate the strength of the author's argument
Remember information more effectively
Make connections between ideas
Supporting Details Tip

When reading, ask yourself: "What evidence does the author provide?" and "How does this detail connect to the main point?"

Inferences

What Are Inferences?

Inferences are logical conclusions drawn from evidence in the text, even when not explicitly stated. They require you to "read between the lines."

Combine textual evidence with background knowledge
Look for implied meanings and suggestions
Consider the author's tone and word choice
Analyze cause-and-effect relationships

Types of Inferences

Different types of inferences serve various purposes:

Character inferences: understanding motivations and traits
Causal inferences: determining cause-and-effect relationships
Predictive inferences: anticipating future events
Evaluative inferences: judging the author's attitude or bias
Example: Making Inferences

Text: "Sarah glanced at her watch for the third time in five minutes, tapped her foot impatiently, and kept looking toward the restaurant entrance."

Inference: Sarah is waiting for someone who is late for their appointment.

Evidence: Checking time repeatedly, impatient body language, watching the entrance.

Making Valid Inferences

To ensure your inferences are accurate:

Base inferences on textual evidence, not assumptions
Consider multiple possible interpretations
Avoid over-inferring beyond what the text supports
Check if your inference is consistent with the overall passage
Inference Tip

Strong inferences feel like logical "next steps" from the evidence. If you have to make big leaps or add information not suggested by the text, reconsider your inference.

Reading Passage Strategies

Types of SAT Reading Passages

The SAT includes several types of passages, each requiring specific reading strategies:

Literature: Fiction excerpts, poetry, or literary nonfiction
Historical Documents: Founding documents, speeches, or historical texts
Social Science: Psychology, sociology, economics, or political science
Natural Science: Biology, chemistry, physics, or earth science
Paired Passages: Two related passages that present different perspectives

Pre-Reading Strategy

Before diving into the passage, use these techniques to orient yourself:

Read the introduction/blurb to understand context and source
Scan the questions first to know what to look for
Note the passage type and adjust your reading approach
Look for any graphics, charts, or visual elements
Literature Passage Strategy
  • Focus on: Character development, mood, tone, literary devices
  • Pay attention to: Dialogue, internal thoughts, descriptive language
  • Ask yourself: What is the character's motivation? How does the author create atmosphere?
  • Common questions: Character analysis, theme identification, literary technique effects
Science Passage Strategy
  • Focus on: Hypotheses, experimental design, results, conclusions
  • Pay attention to: Data, graphs, cause-and-effect relationships
  • Ask yourself: What problem is being investigated? What evidence supports the conclusion?
  • Common questions: Data interpretation, experimental analysis, scientific reasoning

Active Reading Techniques

Engage with the text as you read to improve comprehension and retention:

Underline or circle key terms, names, and dates
Make brief margin notes about main ideas
Mark transitions and signal words (however, therefore, in contrast)
Identify the author's tone and attitude
Note shifts in perspective or argument

Paired Passage Strategies

When dealing with paired passages, use these specific approaches:

Read Passage 1 completely, then answer questions about it
Read Passage 2 completely, then answer questions about it
Finally, tackle questions comparing both passages
Look for areas of agreement and disagreement
Note different approaches to the same topic
Consider how each author would respond to the other's argument
Historical Document Strategy
  • Context is key: Pay attention to when and why the document was written
  • Language barriers: Don't get stuck on archaic language; focus on main ideas
  • Purpose: Identify whether the author is persuading, informing, or arguing
  • Audience: Consider who the intended audience was

Command of Evidence

Many SAT questions ask you to identify textual support for your answers:

Always return to the passage to find specific evidence
Look for direct quotes that support your answer choice
Avoid answers based on outside knowledge
The correct evidence will directly relate to the previous question
Time Management for Reading

Spend about 13 minutes per passage (including questions). If you're running short on time, focus on questions that ask about specific lines or paragraphs rather than those requiring full passage comprehension.

Vocabulary in Context

When encountering unfamiliar words or phrases:

Use context clues from surrounding sentences
Look for definition or explanation within the passage
Consider the word's function in the sentence
Eliminate answer choices that don't fit the context
Remember that SAT often tests secondary meanings of common words
