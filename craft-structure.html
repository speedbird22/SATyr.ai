Text Structure

Common Organizational Patterns

Authors organize information using various structural patterns:

Chronological: events in time order
Cause and effect: showing relationships between events
Compare and contrast: examining similarities and differences
Problem and solution: presenting issues and their remedies
Spatial: describing physical arrangements or locations

Transition Words and Phrases

Transitions signal the relationship between ideas and help identify structure:

Time: first, next, finally, meanwhile, subsequently
Cause/Effect: because, therefore, as a result, consequently
Contrast: however, nevertheless, on the other hand, despite
Addition: furthermore, moreover, in addition
Example: Identifying Structure

Problem-Solution Structure:

"Urban air pollution has reached dangerous levels in many cities. However, several innovative solutions show promise. First, electric vehicle adoption reduces emissions. Additionally, green building standards improve air quality. As a result, cities implementing these measures see significant improvement."

Key transitions: "However" (introduces solution), "First" and "Additionally" (list solutions), "As a result" (shows outcome).

Paragraph Structure

Understanding how paragraphs function within the larger structure:

Topic sentences introduce main ideas
Supporting sentences develop and explain
Concluding sentences summarize or transition
Paragraph breaks signal shifts in focus or time

Rhetorical Strategies

Appeals

Authors use different appeals to persuade their audience:

Logos: appeal to logic and reason (facts, statistics)
Ethos: appeal to credibility and authority (expertise)
Pathos: appeal to emotions (stories, vivid language)

Persuasive Techniques

Authors use various techniques to make their arguments more convincing:

Repetition: reinforcing key ideas
Rhetorical questions: engaging the reader's thought
Anecdotes: illustrating points with stories
Analogies: comparing unfamiliar to familiar
Example: Rhetorical Analysis

Passage: "Our planet is in crisis. Scientists overwhelmingly agree that climate change is real and caused by human activity. We must act now to protect our children's future."

Analysis: Uses logos (scientists agree), pathos (children's future), and ethos (implied authority) to persuade readers.

Word Choice & Tone

Analyzing Diction

Diction refers to the author's choice of words and phrases. These choices create tone and convey meaning beyond the literal definition.

Connotation vs. denotation: implied vs. literal meanings
Formal vs. informal language choices
Technical vs. accessible vocabulary
Emotional impact of specific word choices

Identifying Tone

Tone reflects the author's attitude toward the subject matter or audience:

Objective: neutral, factual presentation
Subjective: personal opinions and emotions
Critical: disapproving or fault-finding
Admiring: positive and respectful
Ironic: saying one thing while meaning another
Example: Word Choice Analysis

Neutral: "The politician made a statement about the policy."

Critical: "The politician issued another empty promise about the failed policy."

Supportive: "The dedicated leader announced a comprehensive solution to address the challenging policy."

Notice how word choice ("statement" vs. "empty promise" vs. "comprehensive solution") creates different tones.

Test-Taking Strategies

Time Management

Effective time management is crucial for SAT success:

Reading Section: 65 minutes for 5 passages (13 minutes per passage)
Writing Section: 35 minutes for 4 passages (8-9 minutes per passage)
Math No Calculator: 25 minutes for 20 questions (1.25 minutes per question)
Math Calculator: 55 minutes for 38 questions (1.4 minutes per question)

Question Order Strategy

Approach questions strategically to maximize your score:

Answer questions in order, but don't get stuck on difficult ones
Mark difficult questions and return to them if time permits
For reading, tackle line reference questions first
Save "best evidence" questions for after you've answered the related question
Process of Elimination (POE)

Step 1: Read the question carefully and identify what it's asking

Step 2: Eliminate obviously wrong answers first

Step 3: Look for extreme language in answer choices (always, never, all, none)

Step 4: Choose the most supported answer, not necessarily the "best" answer

Step 5: When in doubt, choose the most specific and detailed answer

Reading Section Strategies

Specific techniques for the reading section:

Read the passage first, then tackle questions
For line reference questions, read 2-3 lines before and after
Predict answers before looking at choices
Avoid answers that are too extreme or absolute
The correct answer is always supported by the passage

Writing Section Strategies

Approach writing questions systematically:

Read the entire sentence, not just the underlined portion
Look for the most concise, clear option
Check for subject-verb agreement and pronoun consistency
Ensure logical flow and proper transitions
When in doubt, choose "NO CHANGE" if the original seems correct
Math Section Calculator Strategy
  • Use calculator wisely: Not every problem requires it
  • Mental math first: Simple calculations are often faster by hand
  • Check your work: Use calculator to verify complex calculations
  • Graphing features: Use graphing calculator for function problems
  • Store values: Save intermediate results for multi-step problems

Guessing Strategy

There's no penalty for wrong answers, so always guess:

Never leave a question blank
If you can eliminate even one answer choice, guess from the remaining
For math grid-ins, make your best educated guess
Stick with your first instinct unless you find a clear error

Stress Management

Stay calm and focused during the test:

Take deep breaths if you feel overwhelmed
Skip difficult questions and return to them later
Don't panic if you don't know an answer
Focus on the questions you can answer correctly
Remember that you don't need a perfect score
Final Test Day Tips
  • Bring multiple pencils and a working calculator
  • Eat a good breakfast and stay hydrated
  • Arrive early to avoid stress
  • Read all instructions carefully
  • Use the breaks to refresh and refocus

Section-Specific Timing

Detailed timing strategies for each section:

Reading: 2-3 minutes reading, 10-11 minutes on questions
Writing: 1-2 minutes reading, 6-7 minutes on questions
Math No Calc: Spend more time on later, harder questions
Math Calc: Don't rush through easier questions to save time
